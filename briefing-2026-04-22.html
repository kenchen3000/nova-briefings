## 1. Geopolitical & Political News

### Hormuz is now an operating-risk problem, not just a headline-risk problem

The most important new datapoint is Reuters’ report on its world page that **Iran seized two container ships seeking to exit the Gulf via the Strait of Hormuz after firing on them** ([Reuters World](https://www.reuters.com/world/)). That is materially worse than yesterday’s “only three ships passed” style signal because it introduces a more punitive regime: even if some physical traffic exists, counterparties now have to price seizure, insurance, legal, and military risk directly.

At the same time, the market is hearing mixed signals about de-escalation. Trading Economics’ 10Y page says President Trump **extended the ceasefire with Iran indefinitely**, while also noting that the blockade of Hormuz remained and uncertainty persisted after Iran fired on ships ([Trading Economics 10Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)). I am treating the ceasefire extension as directionally helpful but not decisive because the fresh seizure data matter more than diplomatic phrasing right now.

Reuters’ world page also says EU ambassadors approved a **90 billion euro** loan to Ukraine and a new sanctions package against Russia after Hungary lifted its veto ([Reuters World](https://www.reuters.com/world/)). That is not today’s main price driver, but it reinforces the broader point that geopolitical risk premia are not isolated to the Gulf.

### Multi-step reasoning

- **Step 1:** Seizures in Hormuz increase the cost of moving oil even if the waterway is not fully closed.
- **Step 2:** That pushes **Brent to 100.470** and keeps **WTI at 91.465**, meaning the inflation channel is no longer hypothetical ([Trading Economics WTI dashboard](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/wti-crude-oil)).
- **Step 3:** Higher energy costs flow first into inflation expectations and only later into visible demand damage, which is why the **10Y** is still around **4.28%** instead of collapsing on “growth scare” logic ([Trading Economics 10Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)).
- **Step 4:** Equities can still bounce for a session if traders anchor on ceasefire language, but that is fragile because the physical operating environment is getting worse, not better.
- **Step 5:** If seizures continue, the market should migrate from “temporary oil spike” toward “persistent supply tax,” which is worse for cyclicals and long-duration growth than one-day headline relief would suggest.

**Bottom line:** the geopolitical regime worsened today even though the equity tape looks calm. I trust the shipping signal more than the mood signal.

### What the market is really saying

#### Inflation and growth

The official data still say **slowing growth, sticky inflation**. Headline CPI at **3.3% y/y**, PPI at **4.0% y/y**, and core PCE at **3.0% y/y** are too hot for the Fed to ignore, while payrolls at **+178,000**, personal income at **-0.1% m/m**, and industrial production at **-0.5% m/m** say the economy is cooling, not collapsing ([BLS CPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm), [BLS PPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/ppi.nr0.htm), [BLS Employment Situation](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm), [BEA Personal Income and Outlays](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-february-2026), [Fed G.17](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/g17/current/default.htm)).

That mix is exactly why **$100 Brent** matters so much. The economy was already vulnerable to a growth drag. A fresh energy tax hits before any offsetting rate relief arrives.

#### Bonds and the 30Y Treasury thesis

For Ken’s long-duration Treasury thesis, today is still mixed rather than decisively bullish. The **10Y at 4.28%** and **30Y at 4.88%** are not blowing out, which tells you the market still sees slower growth ahead. But they are also not falling enough to say the growth scare is winning over the inflation scare ([Trading Economics 10Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield), [Trading Economics 30Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/30-year-bond-yield)).

The multi-step problem remains:

- slower labor and production data should help bonds,
- but higher oil keeps inflation expectations elevated,
- which delays duration relief,
- and if policy stays fiscally loose while energy stays high, long bonds can remain stuck even as growth weakens.

**My read:** the bond thesis is alive, but it still needs either a cleaner demand break or real oil relief.

#### Housing and real estate

Housing still has a better financing backdrop than a year ago, but not a fresh construction boom. Freddie Mac’s **30-year fixed mortgage rate was 6.30%** on April 16, while the latest official construction release still shows **1.487 million** housing starts and **1.376 million** permits in January ([Freddie Mac PMMS](https://www.freddiemac.com/pmms), [Census New Residential Construction](https://www.census.gov/construction/nrc/current/index.html)).

That is supportive for housing-sensitive assets at the margin, but not enough to overrule the broader inflation and growth mix.

#### Cross-asset message

Today’s cross-asset tape is unusual but interpretable:

- **equities up**,
- **gold lower than yesterday**,

That is not a classic panic. It is a market trying to run a short-term risk-on bounce while still carrying a meaningful commodity and geopolitical risk premium.

## Scenario Analysis

### Theme 1: Hormuz becomes a prolonged seizure-risk corridor

**Base case, 50%:** The waterway remains usable only in a degraded, violent, and politically reversible way, and Brent holds roughly in the **$97-$105** range over the next week ([Trading Economics WTI dashboard](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/wti-crude-oil)).  
**What would change my mind:** several days of visibly normal transit without new seizures or military incidents.

**Bull case, 15%:** Diplomatic signaling overtakes the operating risk, seizures stop, and Brent falls back toward **$92-$97**.  
**What would change my mind:** any additional attack or insurer withdrawal.

**Bear case, 35%:** More seizures or shipping withdrawals push Brent decisively above **$105** and force a broader inflation repricing.  
**What would change my mind:** confirmed normalization in actual shipping, not just political rhetoric.

### Theme 2: Bonds stay trapped between weaker growth and higher oil

**Base case, 50%:** The **10Y** stays roughly in the **4.23%-4.35%** range through this week while traders wait to see whether oil or growth wins ([Trading Economics 10Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)).  
**What would change my mind:** Brent staying above **$105** or a sudden downside shock in labor/spending data.

**Bull case, 20%:** Growth deterioration becomes clearer, the oil spike stabilizes, and the **10Y** pushes toward **4.15%-4.20%**.  
**What would change my mind:** persistent upward pressure in crude.

**Bear case, 30%:** Oil persistence re-anchors inflation expectations and the **10Y** retests **4.35%-4.45%**.  
**What would change my mind:** a visible reversal in shipping stress.

### Theme 3: AI leadership stays narrow

**Base case, 50%:** AI monetizers keep outperforming, but broad tech leadership remains selective because rates do not break lower.  
**What would change my mind:** Brent back below **$95** and the **10Y** moving toward **4.20%**.

**Bull case, 25%:** Ceasefire optimism sticks, rates ease, and AI/megacap leadership broadens again.  
**What would change my mind:** more concrete shipping disruption.

**Bear case, 25%:** Fundamentals remain good, but valuations compress anyway because energy keeps inflation risk alive.  
**What would change my mind:** simultaneous oil relief and softer inflation expectations.
