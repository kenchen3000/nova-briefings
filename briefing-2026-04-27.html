1. Geopolitical and Political News

The market is trading “talks may continue, but the choke point still matters”

The highest-signal geopolitical update is that diplomacy is still functioning, but logistics are still impaired. AP reported Sunday that Iran’s foreign minister briefly returned to Pakistan as mediators tried to revive ceasefire negotiations, while Trump said there was no reason to send envoys and the sides could talk by phone instead. The same AP report said the Strait of Hormuz remains central because a fifth of all traded oil and natural gas passes through it, and that the economic fallout is widening as shipments of oil, LNG, and fertilizer remain disrupted (Local10/AP).

AP’s Friday report had already set up the same problem from a different angle. It said a 90-day Jones Act waiver was extended to ease U.S. oil and gas transport and that Brent fell back toward $104 only after the waiver news, after having traded above $107 earlier. Crucially, AP also said tensions in the strait had not normalized and that Iran kept a “stranglehold” on traffic through the route that carries about one-fifth of global traded oil and gas in peacetime (PBS/AP).

There is also broader regional spillover. The Hindu’s live update, citing multiple reporting streams, said U.S.-Iran peace efforts were still stalled, Araghchi was heading to Moscow after Oman and Pakistan, and Lebanon’s health ministry said Israeli strikes killed 14 people on Sunday, the deadliest day since that truce took effect (The Hindu live update).

Multi-step reasoning

  • If indirect diplomacy continues but shipping restrictions and naval pressure do not normalize, then oil can stay high without a fresh missile headline.
  • High oil then feeds into gasoline, freight, chemicals, fertilizer, airline costs, and inflation expectations.
  • That makes the macro problem worse because the U.S. economy was already slowing at the margin before this shock.
  • If growth softens while energy keeps headline inflation sticky, the Fed has less room to ease quickly.
  • That pushes investors toward the assets with the clearest earnings visibility, which is one reason AI leadership can coexist with a weaker median stock.

Bottom line: the left-tail war panic is lower than it was at the peak, but the operating-cost shock is still live. That is why Monday oil prices matter more than soothing diplomatic phrasing.

Europe and China read-through

Europe still looks more exposed to the energy channel than the U.S. Monday snapshots show DE40 at 24,123, GB100 at 10,354, and France’s 10Y yield at 3.6780, while China’s 10Y yield is 1.7730 and USD/CNY is 6.82414. I read those as “no panic, but imported-energy vulnerability is still real,” especially for Europe (Trading Economics commodities dashboard).

2. AI and Technology

AI earnings still look real enough to offset part of the macro tax

The strongest verified AI datapoints still come from company results, not market storytelling.

Intel’s official release reported Q1 revenue of $13.6 billion, non-GAAP EPS of $0.29, Data Center and AI revenue of $5.1 billion, up 22% y/y, and Q2 revenue guidance of $13.8 billion to $14.8 billion (Intel Q1 2026 results).

TSMC’s latest results also remain strong. Manufacturing Dive, citing company materials, reported Q1 revenue of $35.9 billion, up 40.6% y/y, with Q2 revenue guidance of $39.0 billion to $40.2 billion as AI demand remained robust (Manufacturing Dive on TSMC).

Amazon’s cloud monetization still matters because it says the demand is not just capex, it is already turning into revenue. Reuters reporting, carried by The Hindu, said AWS’s AI revenue run rate exceeded $15 billion in Q1 and Amazon’s chips business run rate exceeded $20 billion (The Hindu/Reuters on Amazon).

The fresh Monday twist is that AI optimism is broadening into devices again. Reuters reported Qualcomm surged on a report that it is working with OpenAI and MediaTek on smartphone processors, a signal that the market still wants new AI demand channels beyond datacenter GPUs (Google News RSS, Reuters/Qualcomm).

Multi-step reasoning

  • Strong TSMC numbers say leading-edge demand is still real and capacity-sensitive, not just hype.
  • Strong Intel numbers say AI inference is broadening the hardware winners, not narrowing them.
  • AWS’s disclosed run rate says the cloud layer is already monetizing, not merely “investing ahead of demand.”
  • That combination helps explain why the Nasdaq complex can absorb part of the oil shock better than the average cyclical stock.
  • But if Brent holds near $108, that is still a tax on non-AI margins, which means AI strength can lift the index while the median business continues to feel pressure.

Bottom line: the AI bull case is still intact, and it is broader than a single-company story, but it is not the same thing as a healthy broad economy.

What the tape is saying

Inflation and growth

The official data still say slowing but not broken growth, paired with too much inflation pressure for comfort. CPI at 3.3% y/y, PPI at 4.0% y/y, and core PCE at 3.0% y/y are not recession numbers, but they are also not a clean path back to easy Fed policy. Meanwhile, payrolls at +178,000, unemployment at 4.3%, and claims at 214,000 say the labor market is cooling only gradually, not cracking (BLS CPI, BLS PPI, BLS Employment Situation, FRED ICSA CSV).

Bonds and duration

The long-duration message is still mixed. The latest official 10Y at 4.34% and 30Y at 4.92% say bonds have not fully embraced a slowdown trade yet, but Monday’s live 4.3190 10Y snapshot also says yields are not exploding higher with oil either (FRED DGS10 CSV, FRED DGS30 CSV, Trading Economics commodities dashboard).

That is exactly what an oil-shock stagflation-lite environment should look like: growth wants yields lower, but energy keeps inflation expectations sticky enough to prevent a clean bond rally.

Housing and credit

Housing is surviving, not reaccelerating. Starts at 1.487 million SAAR are decent, but permits at 1.376 million SAAR and a 6.23% mortgage rate still cap affordability and limit a broad housing upside (Census New Residential Construction, FRED MORTGAGE30US CSV).

Credit still looks calm. A 2.86 high-yield OAS and 6.77% high-yield effective yield are not signaling acute systemic stress, which helps explain why equities are selective rather than outright risk-off (FRED BAMLH0A0HYM2 CSV, FRED BAMLH0A0HYM2EY CSV).

FX, metals, and crypto

A DXY at 98.346 with EUR/USD at 1.17390 says the dollar is not behaving like a full panic haven. Gold at 4,692.26 still shows demand for hard-asset protection, while Bitcoin at $77,884 and Ethereum at $2,313.84 suggest crypto is holding up but not taking over as the main macro stress barometer today (Trading Economics commodities dashboard, CoinGecko simple price API).

Scenario Analysis

Theme 1: Diplomacy continues, but shipping remains functionally impaired

  • Base case (50%): talks continue indirectly, but vessel movement and naval pressure do not normalize quickly, so Brent stays roughly $103 to $110 through this week.
  • Bull case (20%): concrete evidence of shipping normalization pushes Brent back toward $99 to $103.
  • Bear case (30%): talks stall and shipping pressure worsens, pushing Brent above $110 and re-intensifying inflation fears.
  • What would change my mind: verified evidence of restored vessel flow, not just optimistic statements.

Theme 2: AI keeps carrying the index while the median stock lags

  • Base case (50%): semis and AI infrastructure keep outperforming because earnings support is real, while non-AI cyclicals stay mixed.
  • Bull case (25%): this week’s megacap earnings plus softer yields broaden leadership beyond AI.
  • Bear case (25%): elevated oil and sticky rates squeeze margins enough that even strong AI results cannot stop broader market weakness.
  • What would change my mind: a sustained Brent move below $100 plus a 10Y closer to 4.20% would make broadening much more believable.

Theme 3: Long duration remains a patience trade, not a clean breakout

  • Base case (55%): the 10Y stays roughly 4.25% to 4.38% through the PCE and payroll releases as inflation and growth signals offset each other.
  • Bull case (20%): activity data soften materially and yields move toward 4.20% to 4.25%.
  • Bear case (25%): another oil leg higher pushes the 10Y above 4.38% to 4.40%.
  • What would change my mind: either a visible labor-market break or a clean oil retracement.

Investment Outlook

Favorable vs. unfavorable

More favorable: - selective semiconductors and AI infrastructure with clear revenue conversion, - energy producers and services while Brent stays elevated, - gold and selective inflation hedges, - patient duration exposure only if growth softens further.

Less favorable: - low-quality cyclicals, - broad small-cap beta, - consumer-margin stories without pricing power, - rate-sensitive sectors that need both lower yields and lower oil at the same time.

Risks to watch or hedge

  1. Oil plateau risk: a long stay near $108 Brent is more dangerous than a brief spike because it feeds margins and inflation expectations repeatedly.
  2. False breadth risk: the index can look healthy while the median company is weakening under energy and financing pressure.
  3. Event-week risk: BEA’s next PCE release is due April 30, and if it comes in hotter than the market expects, the “Fed can look through oil” narrative weakens quickly (BEA Personal Income and Outlays).

Contrarian view

The contrarian bullish case is that markets are correctly looking through the shock because credit is calm, the labor market is not breaking, and AI monetization is real. That view has evidence behind it. But I think it applies much more to selective megacap and semiconductor exposure than to the broad economy. If Brent stays above $105, the median business still faces a real input-cost squeeze.
