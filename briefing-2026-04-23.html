## 1. Geopolitical & Political News

### Hormuz is still the story, and it is getting more economically toxic

The highest-signal geopolitical datapoint this morning remains the same core fact, but worse in implication: Reuters-syndicated reporting says **Iran seized two ships in the Strait of Hormuz on Wednesday**, one day after Trump called off renewed attacks without actually restoring negotiations or lifting the U.S. maritime blockade of Iranian trade ([Reuters via Internazionale](https://www.internazionale.it/ultime-notizie-reuters/2026/04/23/iran-seizes-ships-in-strait-of-hormuz-after-us-calls-off-renewed-attacks)). SCMP separately reports Iran **fired on three ships and seized two**, and describes the strait as effectively choking off nearly all exports through a route that normally carries about **20% of the world’s traded oil** ([SCMP](https://www.scmp.com/news/world/middle-east/article/3351022/iran-fires-3-ships-strait-hormuz-after-trump-extends-ceasefire)).

What matters most is not just that ships were seized. It is that this confirms a more hostile operating regime:

- passage is no longer merely reduced,
- counterparties now have to price seizure and hostile-interdiction risk,
- and a ceasefire headline is not restoring commercial confidence.

That distinction matters because markets can often fade military rhetoric. They cannot fade an operating regime in which shipping, insurance, and legal permission are all impaired at once.

### Multi-step reasoning

- **Step 1:** Vessel seizures and near-shutdown traffic raise the cost of physically moving oil, even before any new formal sanctions.
- **Step 2:** That keeps **Brent above $100** and **WTI near $94**, which directly re-inflates the energy pass-through channel ([Reuters Markets](https://www.reuters.com/markets/), [Trading Economics WTI](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/wti-crude-oil)).
- **Step 3:** Because the U.S. macro backdrop was already sticky on inflation, the first market response is not “buy bonds,” but “delay cuts and hold yields up,” which is exactly what the **4.29% to 4.309% 10Y** is signaling ([Trading Economics 10Y](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield), [Reuters Markets](https://www.reuters.com/markets/)).
- **Step 4:** Higher discount rates then hit long-duration and narrative-heavy equities first, while energy-linked cash flows and defensive balance sheets hold up relatively better.
- **Step 5:** Only later, if the shock persists, does the market start to price more obvious demand destruction and recession risk.

**Bottom line:** this is still primarily an inflation-shock sequence, not a clean recession sequence. That is why long bonds are not rallying hard, and why equities are weakening only selectively rather than capitulating.

### Europe and China read-through

Europe remains vulnerable because the energy transmission channel is fastest there. Reuters’ world page was thin in this run, but the broader implication is unchanged: if Hormuz stays impaired, Europe imports the shock quickly through energy costs and industrial margins.

China is exposed through both energy costs and trade-route uncertainty. I did **not** verify a fresh official Chinese macro release this morning, so I am keeping this as a directional exposure point rather than inventing a new hard datapoint.

### What the market is really saying

#### Inflation and growth

The official macro data still say **growth is softening, but inflation is not solved**. CPI at **3.3% y/y**, PPI at **4.0% y/y**, and core PCE at **3.0% y/y** are all too hot to welcome an energy shock ([BLS CPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm), [BLS PPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/ppi.nr0.htm), [BEA Personal Income and Outlays](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-february-2026)). At the same time, payroll growth of **178,000**, unemployment of **4.3%**, personal income at **-0.1% m/m**, and industrial production at **-0.5% m/m** say the economy was already losing momentum before the latest shipping escalation ([BLS Employment Situation](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm), [BEA Personal Income and Outlays](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-february-2026), [Fed G.17](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/g17/current/default.htm)).

That is the worst combination for policy: not weak enough to force immediate cuts, not cool enough on inflation to absorb a fresh oil spike.

#### Bonds and the 30Y Treasury thesis

For Ken’s long-duration Treasury thesis, this is still a **mixed** setup. The **10Y at roughly 4.29% to 4.309%** and the **30Y at 4.90%** are not panicking higher, which tells you the market still believes slower growth will matter eventually ([Trading Economics 10Y](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield), [Reuters Markets](https://www.reuters.com/markets/), [Trading Economics 30Y](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/30-year-bond-yield)). But they are also not breaking lower in the way a clean bond-bull setup would require.

The chain is the problem:

- weaker growth should help bonds,
- but higher oil lifts inflation expectations,
- which delays any easing impulse,
- and if fiscal policy stays supportive or geopolitical risk keeps energy elevated, long duration can stay trapped.

**My read:** the 30Y thesis is alive, but it still needs either clearer demand damage or visible oil relief. It does **not** yet have either.

## Scenario Analysis

### Theme 1: Hormuz stays in seizure-risk mode

**Base case, 55%:** The waterway remains partially usable only under coercive and reversible conditions, and Brent stays roughly in the **$100-$106** range over the next week ([Reuters Markets](https://www.reuters.com/markets/), [Trading Economics WTI](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/wti-crude-oil)).  
**What would change my mind:** several days of normal commercial transit with no new seizures, no firing incidents, and visible insurer confidence returning.

**Bull case, 15%:** Back-channel diplomacy actually restores traffic confidence and Brent falls back toward **$95-$100**.  
**What would change my mind:** any additional seizures, mining threats, or signs that the blockade remains commercially binding.

**Bear case, 30%:** The route remains effectively frozen, Brent pushes above **$106**, and markets shift from “inflation-risk repricing” toward a broader stagflation scare.  
**What would change my mind:** credible de-escalation that changes actual traffic, not just rhetoric.

### Theme 2: Bonds remain trapped between weak growth and energy inflation

**Base case, 50%:** The **10Y** stays roughly in the **4.25%-4.35%** range through April 24 while the market waits to see whether oil persistence or growth softness wins ([Trading Economics 10Y](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)).  
**What would change my mind:** Brent moving decisively above **$106** or a very weak hard-growth datapoint.

**Bull case, 20%:** Growth concerns reassert themselves, oil stabilizes, and the **10Y** drifts toward **4.15%-4.22%**.  
**What would change my mind:** continued Brent strength above **$100**.

**Bear case, 30%:** Energy persistence re-anchors inflation expectations and the **10Y** retests **4.35%-4.45%**.  
**What would change my mind:** a visible normalization in Hormuz traffic.

### Theme 3: AI leadership stays narrow

**Base case, 50%:** AI monetizers and semicap leaders hold up better than the broader growth complex, but leadership remains selective because rates stay high.  
**What would change my mind:** Brent back below **$97** and the **10Y** moving closer to **4.20%**.

**Bull case, 20%:** Oil retreats and broad tech leadership reopens quickly.  
**What would change my mind:** another negative shipping headline.

**Bear case, 30%:** Fundamentals remain strong, but valuations compress anyway because macro stays hostile.  
**What would change my mind:** simultaneous oil relief and softer inflation expectations.

## Investment Outlook

### Directional calls

- **More favorable:** energy cash-flow exposure, selective quality AI/megacap with visible monetization, and patient long-duration Treasuries only if oil shows real signs of topping.
- **Less favorable:** low-quality cyclicals, broad small-cap beta, and any thesis that assumes the Gulf disruption is already fading.
- **Worth hedging:** inflation re-acceleration risk. Brent above **$102** is high enough to matter even if growth keeps slowing ([Reuters Markets](https://www.reuters.com/markets/), [Trading Economics WTI](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/wti-crude-oil)).
- **For the 30Y Treasury thesis:** stay open, but do not confuse “yields are not blowing out” with “the bond bull case is confirmed.”

### Contrarian view

The contrarian bullish view is that equities are still basically right: if the **S&P 500** is only down modestly and the **10Y** is still near **4.30%**, maybe markets are correctly treating this as a temporary logistics shock rather than a lasting macro regime change ([Reuters Markets](https://www.reuters.com/markets/), [Trading Economics 10Y](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)).

That view has some merit. I still think it is too relaxed. The route is not merely “tense”; it is operating under coercion. The longer that persists, the more likely inflation expectations and margin pressure outrun the market’s current calm.
