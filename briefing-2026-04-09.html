1. Geopolitical & Political News

Ceasefire is holding just enough to avoid panic, but not enough to restore confidence

Trading Economics reports that renewed Israeli strikes in Lebanon and continued disputes over whether the truce extends to Lebanon are reviving doubts about the ceasefire’s durability. Iranian officials also accused the U.S. of violating the agreement, while U.S. Vice President JD Vance is reportedly leading a delegation to Islamabad for direct talks with Iran this weekend (Trading Economics, Brent, Trading Economics, US500).

Why this matters:

1. The market is shifting from “war tomorrow” to “messy truce.” That is better than outright escalation, but it does not justify fully removing the risk premium from oil.

2. Hormuz remains the transmission mechanism. Trading Economics still describes the strait as largely obstructed, so even with talks alive, energy logistics remain fragile (Trading Economics, Brent).

3. Political incentives cut both ways. Washington wants a lower-oil, lower-inflation narrative into CPI. Tehran wants leverage before talks. That makes small violations more likely than total peace.

Multi-step reasoning

  • Step 1: Ceasefire headline removes the immediate worst-case strike premium.
  • Step 2: Oil collapses for one day because traders unwind disaster hedges.
  • Step 3: Once Brent reaches the low-to-mid 90s, any sign of truce fragility brings buyers back because physical supply risk was never actually resolved.
  • Step 4: That oil rebound limits how far Treasury yields can fall.
  • Step 5: That in turn limits the upside for expensive growth equities.
  • Step 6: If CPI tomorrow is still tame despite the oil turmoil, markets may decide the geopolitical shock is a temporary level effect rather than a regime change.

The key point is this: geopolitics is now influencing markets through inflation expectations more than through direct fear alone.

2. AI & Technology

No fresh AI catalyst today, but the Fed minutes matter more than usual

The March FOMC minutes explicitly noted that earlier in the intermeeting period, concerns about AI disruptions to some business models weighed on equities and interest-rate expectations, and that software underperformed the broader market again over the period (FOMC Minutes, Mar. 17-18, 2026).

That matters because it confirms two things:

1. AI is no longer a one-way macro positive in policymaker and investor psychology. It is also being treated as a disruption risk to incumbents.

2. The current AI trade remains heavily rate-sensitive. If rates fall, the sector gets multiple expansion. If oil keeps inflation sticky, the sector loses that tailwind fast.

For Ken’s long-30Y thesis, that means the setup is improving tactically, but the cleaner validation still requires softer inflation or clearer growth deterioration. We have neither yet.

#### Oil

Brent at $98.39 and WTI at $100.307 are the most important numbers this morning (Trading Economics, Brent, Trading Economics dashboard). The market is saying the immediate catastrophe premium came out yesterday, but the underlying energy risk premium is still alive.

That means the inflation story has changed from “imminent spike” to “still elevated baseline.” That is a much better setup for equities than $109 Brent, but it is still not the same as a full return to benign 2025-style oil conditions.

#### Equities

The US500 at 6,778.99 is effectively flat, which is meaningful because it follows a strong relief day and then fails to extend materially (Trading Economics, US500). When relief rallies are real, they typically broaden on day two. When they stall immediately, it usually means investors want to see the next hard data point, which in this case is CPI.

My actual read

The biggest mistake this morning would be to read “ceasefire” as “resolution.” The market is not doing that. Oil nearly at $100, flat equities, and a slightly firmer 10Y all say the same thing: tail risk fell, but inflation risk did not disappear.

For Ken’s bond thesis, I’m more constructive than I was on April 7, but not ready to say the hard part is over. The best setup for long bonds now is not just geopolitical calm, it is geopolitical calm plus soft CPI. Without both, the thesis improves only in steps.
