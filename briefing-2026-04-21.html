1. Geopolitical & Political News

Hormuz is moving toward choke-point conditions again

The most important fresh datapoint this morning is Reuters’ report that **shipping traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remained broadly halted on Tuesday, with only three ships passing the waterway in the past 24 hours** ([Reuters World](https://www.reuters.com/world/)). Reuters’ world page also says Trump told CNBC he **did not want to extend a ceasefire with Iran**, while separately saying the U.S. was negotiating from strength ([Reuters World](https://www.reuters.com/world/)).

That combination matters. The market is no longer dealing with “headline de-escalation” only. It is dealing with a regime where:

  • the legal and military environment is still unstable,
  • actual ship traffic is constrained,
  • and political leaders are talking as if pressure will continue rather than ease.

Fed and policy angle

Reuters’ world page also says **former Fed Governor Kevin Warsh** faces a Senate Banking Committee hearing on Tuesday as part of the process to become Fed chair ([Reuters World](https://www.reuters.com/world/)). That does not move today’s oil barrel directly, but it does matter for rate sensitivity. When inflation risk is being driven by energy and geopolitics, even modest shifts in perceived future Fed reaction functions can move long-duration assets disproportionately.

Europe and China read-through

Europe remains the obvious external vulnerability because energy is the fastest transmission channel. China is also exposed because shipping impairment and higher energy costs hit industrial margins and trade flows before they show up in headline GDP. I did **not** verify fresh official Europe or China macro releases this morning, so I am keeping this read qualitative rather than inventing a data point.

Multi-step reasoning

  • **Step 1:** Lower vessel flow means actual tradable oil supply is more constrained than Monday’s middle-ground narrative suggested.
  • **Step 2:** That keeps **Brent near 95.54** instead of falling back into the high **$80s** ([Trading Economics WTI dashboard](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/wti-crude-oil)).
  • **Step 3:** Oil in the mid-**$90s** keeps inflation expectations sticky enough that the **10Y** stays around **4.28%**, even with growth data already softening ([Trading Economics 10Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)).
  • **Step 4:** Higher-for-longer discount rates cap speculative duration first, but not necessarily quality cash-generating equities, which is why the **US500** is up **0.39%** while leadership is more selective under the surface ([Trading Economics US500](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/stock-market)).
  • **Step 5:** If traders conclude this is still a contained supply shock rather than a broad economic freeze, energy can stay bid while equities remain resilient. That is exactly the awkward mix the market is showing this morning.

**Bottom line:** the political regime has worsened faster than the equity tape has acknowledged. That does **not** mean equities must crash today. It **does** mean the inflation-risk tail is still underpriced if ship flow remains this weak.

What the market is really saying

#### Inflation and growth

The hard macro data still say **sticky inflation plus softer activity**, not a clean recession and not a clean disinflation win. Headline CPI at **3.3% y/y**, PPI at **4.0% y/y**, and core PCE at **3.0% y/y** are all too hot for comfort, while payroll growth at **178,000**, personal income at **-0.1% m/m**, and industrial production at **-0.5% m/m** say the economy is losing speed without fully breaking ([BLS CPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/cpi.nr0.htm), [BLS PPI](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/ppi.nr0.htm), [BLS Employment Situation](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm), [BEA Personal Income and Outlays](https://www.bea.gov/news/2026/personal-income-and-outlays-february-2026), [Fed G.17](https://www.federalreserve.gov/releases/g17/current/default.htm)).

That is why higher oil matters so much. A softening economy can help bonds **only if** inflation expectations keep easing. Mid-**$90s** Brent threatens that relief channel.

#### Bonds and the 30Y Treasury thesis

For Ken’s long-duration Treasury thesis, today is still a **wait-and-see** setup. The **30Y** at **4.89%** and **10Y** at **4.28%** are below true panic territory, but they are not breaking lower hard enough to say the market has chosen the growth-scare path ([Trading Economics 30Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/30-year-bond-yield), [Trading Economics 10Y Treasury](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/government-bond-yield)).

The multi-step issue is:

  • if AI and broader growth slow the labor market, bonds should benefit,
  • but if Washington responds with fiscal support while oil stays high, the inflation impulse can dominate,
  • and if the supply shock is geopolitical rather than cyclical, long bonds can stay stuck longer than growth bulls expect.

**My read:** the long-bond thesis is still alive, but it needs either **clearer growth deterioration** or **clearer oil relief**. Right now it has neither.

#### Housing and real estate

Freddie Mac says the average **30-year fixed mortgage rate was 6.30% as of April 16, 2026**, down from **6.37%** the prior week and **6.83%** a year earlier ([Freddie Mac PMMS](https://www.freddiemac.com/pmms)). The latest official construction release available this morning still shows **January** starts at **1.487 million SAAR** and permits at **1.376 million SAAR** ([Census New Residential Construction](https://www.census.gov/construction/nrc/current/index.html)).

That says housing has a **better financing backdrop than a year ago**, but not enough fresh official construction data to call a new housing acceleration.

#### Cross-asset message

This morning’s mix is coherent:

  • **oil high**,
  • **yields sticky**,
  • **equities resilient**,
  • **gold softer**,
  • **bitcoin steady**.

That is not a classic risk-off panic. It is a market saying: **we believe the supply shock is real, but we are not yet convinced it will crush growth or force immediate policy tightening.**

**Base case, 50%:** Hormuz remains severely impaired but not fully sealed, and Brent holds roughly in the **$93-$99** range over the next week.

**What would change my mind:** several days of visibly improved ship traffic or credible diplomatic de-escalation.

**Base case, 50%:** The **10Y** stays roughly in the **4.22%-4.35%** range through this week as markets wait for either cleaner growth damage or cleaner oil relief.

Directional calls

Contrarian view

The contrarian bullish view is that equities are right and shipping headlines are lagging noise: if the **US500** is still at **7,136.66**, maybe the market is correctly looking through the disruption ([Trading Economics US500](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/stock-market)). That view has some merit because earnings resilience is real.

I still think it is too complacent. Physical-flow evidence deteriorated today, not improved. Markets can ignore that for a bit, but they cannot ignore it forever if ship counts stay this low.
