1. Geopolitical & Political News

The weekend story is not peace, it is escalation after a failed test of reopening

The market saw the first meaningful outward tanker movement through Hormuz on April 11. The Liberia-flagged **Serifos** and China-flagged **Cospearl Lake** and **He Rong Hai** passed through the “Hormuz Passage trial anchorage,” and each vessel is capable of carrying **2 million barrels** of oil ([Straits Times/Reuters, tanker transit](https://www.straitstimes.com/world/middle-east/supertankers-exit-gulf-via-strait-of-hormuz-as-us-iran-talks-begin-data-shows)). Two of those cargoes were chartered by Unipec, the trading arm of Sinopec, which is important because it suggests Chinese buyers were among the first to test the route again ([Straits Times/Reuters, tanker transit](https://www.straitstimes.com/world/middle-east/supertankers-exit-gulf-via-strait-of-hormuz-as-us-iran-talks-begin-data-shows)).

That looked like the start of a fragile normalization. Then the talks in Pakistan failed, and Trump announced on April 12 that the U.S. Navy would begin blockading the strait, interdicting vessels that paid tolls to Iran, while also moving to destroy Iranian mines. The strait handles about **20% of global energy supplies** ([Straits Times/Reuters, blockade](https://www.straitstimes.com/world/middle-east/trump-vows-to-blockade-strait-of-hormuz-after-iran-peace-talks-stumble)).

Europe and China read-through

  • **Europe:** EUR/USD finished at **1.1720** on April 10, near a late-February high, with markets also responding to peace optimism around Russia-Ukraine as well as a wait-and-see posture into the US-Iran weekend talks ([Trading Economics, EUR/USD](https://tradingeconomics.com/euro-area/currency)).
  • **China:** USD/CNY finished at **6.8202** on April 10. Trading Economics notes China’s March CPI slowed to **1.0% y/y** from **1.3%**, while producer prices rose **0.5% y/y**, the first increase since September 2022, partly due to higher global energy costs ([Trading Economics, China currency page](https://tradingeconomics.com/china/currency)).

Why this matters

1. **The world just moved from “reopen by consent” to “reopen by force.”** That is a fundamentally different market regime.

2. **China is still the key marginal buyer to watch.** If Chinese-chartered cargoes keep moving even under this new U.S. posture, oil may stay below crisis levels. If they stop, the price ceiling jumps.

3. **Europe gets the disinflation benefit only if this stays contained.** A higher EUR/USD and softer dollar help imported inflation at the margin, but not if oil gaps higher first.

Multi-step reasoning

  • **Step 1:** Saturday’s tanker passages told the market that some actors were willing to test the route again.
  • **Step 2:** Sunday’s blockade announcement tells the market the route is still politically unresolved.
  • **Step 3:** If insurers, shippers, and Asian buyers treat the blockade as safer than Iranian toll collection, oil may only rise modestly.
  • **Step 4:** But if the blockade creates legal uncertainty, naval confrontation risk, or retaliatory mining and missile threats, shipping activity freezes again.
  • **Step 5:** Frozen shipping lifts oil, which hardens inflation expectations, which pressures the long end of the Treasury curve, which then compresses equity multiples, especially for expensive duration-sensitive equities.
  • **Step 6:** That tighter market backdrop feeds back into politics, increasing pressure either for a rapid diplomatic patch or a broader military confrontation.

The important distinction is this: **the oil market can tolerate risk, but it cannot tolerate operational ambiguity for long.**
