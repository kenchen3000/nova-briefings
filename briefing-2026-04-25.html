1. Geopolitical and Political News

Diplomacy improved, but the market still does not believe the choke point is solved

The key geopolitical development is not a clean de-escalation. It is a fragile shift from “no talks” to “possible talks.” AP, via PBS, reported Friday that Iran’s foreign minister Abbas Araghchi was heading to Pakistan as Islamabad tried to restart U.S.-Iran negotiations. The same report said the White House also extended a Jones Act waiver by 90 days to ease oil and gas transport into U.S. ports, and noted Brent retreated toward $104/bbl on that news after earlier moving above $107/bbl (PBS/AP).

But the same AP report also said the strait remained under severe pressure, with Iran keeping a “stranglehold” on traffic and the U.S. maintaining pressure on Iranian ports (PBS/AP). Reuters/LSEG market data confirm traders are not treating this as resolved: Brent was 105.88, gold 4,714.40, EUR/USD 1.1720, and the US 10Y 4.306% when I pulled the page (Reuters Markets).

Europe and China read-through

Europe still looks more exposed to the energy channel than the U.S. Reuters’ market page showed the STOXX down 0.58% and FTSE down 0.75%, while Trading Economics showed Germany’s DE40 down 0.11% and China’s Shanghai index down 0.33% (Reuters Markets, Trading Economics commodities dashboard). China’s imported-energy vulnerability also shows up in rates and FX, with USD/CNY at 6.83404 and China’s government-bond yield at 1.7610% on Trading Economics (Trading Economics commodities dashboard).

Multi-step reasoning

  • If Pakistan-mediated talks create only headline progress, not shipping normalization, then oil stays high even without a new military shock.
  • High oil acts first as a margin tax on transport, chemicals, airlines, logistics, and consumer discretionary.
  • That weakens the non-AI part of the economy before it necessarily breaks the megacap part of the equity market.
  • If growth softens while headline inflation stays sticky, the Fed does not get a clean reason to cut quickly.
  • That in turn keeps duration only partially supported, which is why the 10Y is stable near 4.31%, not collapsing (Trading Economics 10Y, Reuters Markets).
  • The feedback loop is important: oil shock -> sticky inflation expectations -> delayed easing -> weaker cyclicals -> more crowding into AI defensives/growth leaders.

Bottom line: diplomacy is helping cap the upside tail in oil, but markets are still paying a very real geopolitical insurance premium.

2. AI and Technology

Friday’s real story was not just Nvidia, it was broadening AI participation

The biggest new tech signal is that the AI rally broadened from pure GPU winners into the CPU and semiconductor stack. Reuters reporting said Intel’s first-quarter demand from AI-service firms was strong enough that it sold chips it had originally written off. Intel stock surged more than 24% to about $83 in early Friday trading, taking market value above $416 billion, while AMD and Arm each gained more than 11% (Reuters via WHBL, Intel). A separate Reuters report said the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index rose 3.2% to an all-time high and is up more than 47% this year (Reuters via WHBL, chip index).

This does not invalidate Nvidia. It changes the market structure. If inference and agentic workloads keep shifting value toward CPUs, networking, memory, and foundry capacity, then AI monetization becomes broader, which is bullish for semis as a group.

That broader demand is consistent with TSMC’s latest numbers. Manufacturing Dive, citing TSMC’s earnings materials, reported Q1 revenue of $35.9 billion, up 40.6% y/y, with Q2 revenue guidance of $39.0 billion to $40.2 billion (Manufacturing Dive on TSMC). Amazon’s AI monetization also still looks real rather than narrative-only: Reuters reporting carried by The Hindu said AWS AI revenue run rate exceeded $15 billion in Q1 and Amazon’s chips business run rate exceeded $20 billion (The Hindu/Reuters on Amazon).

Trading Economics’ Friday market wrap matched that tone, saying Intel surged 23%, Nvidia rose more than 4%, Amazon added more than 3%, and the Nasdaq climbed 1.6% to a record close (Trading Economics US500).

Multi-step reasoning

  • Strong TSMC numbers say leading-edge demand is still supply-constrained and real.
  • Amazon’s AI run-rate disclosure says hyperscaler monetization is not purely future tense.
  • Intel’s surge says the market is repricing the idea that AI inference is not a single-company trade.
  • That broadening reduces concentration risk inside tech, but it does not remove macro risk for the rest of the market.
  • If oil remains above $100, the likely outcome is not “AI down.” It is “AI holds up while cyclicals lag,” which actually widens market concentration again after a brief broadening burst.

Bottom line: the AI bull case is still intact, and Friday made it broader, but it is still earnings-backed leadership, not a universal green light for risk.

What the tape is saying

Inflation and growth

The macro combination is still late-cycle, not recessionary. Inflation is too sticky for comfort, especially after March energy spikes, but activity is no longer convincingly accelerating. CPI at 3.3% y/y, PPI at 4.0% y/y, and core PCE at 3.0% y/y do not give the Fed a clean disinflation story. At the same time, payrolls at +178,000, unemployment at 4.3%, personal spending at +0.5% m/m, and claims still near historically healthy levels say the economy is slowing, not cracking (BLS CPI, BLS PPI, BLS Employment Situation, BEA Personal Income and Outlays).

Bonds and duration

For the long-duration Treasury thesis, the good news is that the 10Y did not break upward with oil. The bad news is that it also did not break down. That is exactly what a “soft growth, sticky inflation” world should look like. If oil remains high, inflation expectations hold up just enough to keep long bonds from fully rallying. If growth worsens materially, duration should eventually win. Right now, the market is still waiting to see which channel dominates.

Housing and real estate

Housing is stabilizing at a slow pace, not booming. Starts at 1.487 million SAAR were better than permits at 1.376 million SAAR, but a 6.23% mortgage rate still caps affordability and turnover (Census New Residential Construction, FRED MORTGAGE30US). That is more supportive for existing-home scarcity than for a broad housing-cycle reacceleration.

Credit and risk appetite

High yield spreads are still relatively contained at 2.86, with high-yield effective yield at 6.79% as of April 23 (FRED BAMLH0A0HYM2, FRED BAMLH0A0HYM2EY). That matters. Credit is not confirming a deep risk-off event. So the current message is: macro tax is real, but the system is not yet financially stressed.

Investment Outlook

Favorable vs. unfavorable

More favorable:

  • selective semiconductors and AI infrastructure with visible monetization,
  • energy cash-flow exposure while Brent remains above $100,
  • patient duration exposure only if growth data soften further without another oil spike.

Less favorable:

  • low-quality cyclicals,
  • broad small-cap beta,
  • rate-sensitive sectors that need both lower yields and lower oil to outperform,
  • any thesis that assumes the Hormuz shock is already economically irrelevant.

Risks to watch or hedge

1. Oil persistence risk: the market can digest a short spike better than a long plateau. 2. Margin compression outside megacap tech: AI leaders can carry indexes only so far. 3. Fed inertia: sticky inflation plus soft growth is the hardest mix for policy and for long-duration timing.

Contrarian view

The contrarian bullish view is that the market is correctly looking through the shock. There is real evidence for that view: credit spreads are contained, the S&P 500 is at a record, the Nasdaq is making new highs, and AI revenue is clearly monetizing (FRED BAMLH0A0HYM2, Reuters Markets, Trading Economics US500, The Hindu/Reuters on Amazon).

I think that view has merit, but only selectively. The market is currently treating AI earnings strength as an offset to a geopolitical supply tax. That is believable for megacap tech and semis. It is less believable for the median company and the median consumer if crude stays elevated into May.
