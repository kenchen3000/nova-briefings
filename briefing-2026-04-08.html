What changed

1. The binary catalyst resolved in the market-friendly direction. Instead of following through on the “power plants and bridges” threat, Trump delayed it two weeks and markets interpreted that as a de-escalation window tied to temporary Hormuz reopening (Trading Economics, Brent, Trading Economics, 10Y).

2. The inflation shock unwound fast. Brent dropped from $109.27 to $92.26 in one session, which mechanically eases near-term inflation expectations and reduces pressure on rates (Trading Economics, Brent).

3. Risk appetite came back immediately. US500 rose to 6,784.35, and Trading Economics reports speculative AI stocks and airlines among the biggest beneficiaries of the oil collapse and de-escalation headline (Trading Economics, US500).

4. The macro floor is still labor-market resilience. March nonfarm payrolls increased 178,000 and unemployment remained 4.3%, so the economy is not entering this de-escalation window from a position of recessionary weakness (BLS Employment Situation).

Lessons from the miss

Prediction #28 was wrong because I overweighted public rhetoric and underweighted Trump’s pattern of deadline extension when markets and diplomacy both offer an off-ramp. The key correction is this: when he escalates publicly but there is still a plausible negotiating channel, the first move is often delay, not detonation. I need to put more weight on “credibility damage is tolerable if markets rally” and less weight on “public threat forces follow-through.”

1. Geopolitical & Political News

The key development: strike threat postponed, negotiation window opened

The market consensus this morning is clear from price action: the US did not launch the feared immediate infrastructure strike. Trading Economics summarizes the move as Trump agreeing to a two-week pause while calling Iran’s latest proposal a “workable basis for negotiations,” with Iran temporarily allowing shipping through the Strait of Hormuz (Trading Economics, Brent).

Why this matters:

1. Immediate tail risk fell sharply. Yesterday’s market had to price a binary overnight strike risk. Today that specific tail risk is lower.

2. But the underlying conflict is not solved. A two-week pause is not a peace treaty. It is a probation period.

3. Hormuz is the real transmission channel. If shipping normalizes even temporarily, oil can fall much faster than war headlines fade, because the physical supply panic is what was driving the inflation scare.

4. Trump preserved optionality. He avoided backing down completely, because the threat is delayed rather than withdrawn. Politically, that matters.

Multi-step reasoning

  • Step 1: Threat delayed rather than executed.
  • Step 2: Oil collapses because the worst-case supply disruption is repriced lower.
  • Step 3: Lower oil reduces inflation fear and lowers Treasury yields.
  • Step 4: Lower yields support duration-sensitive assets and speculative growth equities.
  • Step 5: Stronger markets reduce White House pressure to escalate immediately.
  • Step 6: That easier market reaction itself increases the odds Trump keeps negotiating, unless Iran or a proxy re-breaks the equilibrium.

That feedback loop is the big change. Yesterday the loop was escalation -> inflation -> tighter financial conditions -> more political stress. This morning the loop is pause -> lower oil -> easier financial conditions -> more room for diplomacy.

What could still go wrong

The de-escalation is fragile. Trading Economics also notes a drone strike on Saudi Arabia’s East-West pipeline in the same broad window (Trading Economics, Brent). I am not treating that as independently verified beyond that source, but it is a reminder that the market’s biggest vulnerability is not a failed headline, it is a fresh supply disruption.

2. AI & Technology

AI is behaving like a high-beta macro trade again

This morning’s price action says AI leadership is back on, at least tactically. Trading Economics reports that Nvidia, Meta, Tesla, AMD, and Micron were up roughly 4% to 10% pre-market as the de-escalation trade hit, while airlines also rallied on the improved fuel outlook (Trading Economics, US500).

Interpretation:

  • This is not a new AI fundamental catalyst.
  • It is a macro duration and energy-cost relief trade.
  • Cheaper oil matters for data-center and logistics-heavy capex narratives at the margin, but the bigger driver is simply that falling yields re-expand the valuation multiple investors will pay for AI-linked growth.

Multi-step reasoning

  • Lower oil -> lower inflation scare.
  • Lower inflation scare -> lower bond yields.
  • Lower bond yields -> higher present value for long-duration growth.
  • AI leaders are the most crowded long-duration growth trade in the market.
  • Therefore, they rebound first and hardest when the energy-shock thesis cracks.

Contrarian caution: if this de-escalation fails, the same names reverse first. This is relief-driven leadership, not new information about AI demand.

Bonds: this is the first real relief for Ken’s long-duration thesis in days

The 10-year yield easing to 4.27% matters more than the absolute move suggests (Trading Economics, 10Y). The important point is not 3 basis points by itself. It is that the market is re-pricing the direction of the next macro shock.

Yesterday’s fear was: war escalation -> oil spike -> inflation shock -> no cuts.

Today’s pricing is: ceasefire window -> oil collapse -> inflation scare eases -> cuts back on the table.

That said, this is supportive, not decisive, for the long-bond thesis. The labor market is still firm, not recessionary. BLS still shows 178,000 payroll growth and 4.3% unemployment in March (BLS Employment Situation). So bonds are getting geopolitical relief, not yet a clean growth slowdown signal.

Oil: the market is saying supply panic was overextended

A one-day move from $109.27 to $92.26 in Brent is not a normal fluctuation. It is the market admitting that the prior price included a substantial immediate-war premium that has now been removed (Trading Economics, Brent).

So what does this mean?

  • Energy equities lose their best near-term tailwind.
  • Airline and transport margins improve fast.
  • Headline inflation risk for April softens materially if the move holds.
  • Consumer sentiment gets breathing room, even if gasoline passthrough lags.

Equities: relief rally, but not a clean all-clear

US500 at 6,784.35 still sits below the January all-time high of 7,002.58, which Trading Economics lists for January 2026 (Trading Economics, US500). That means this is a strong relief rally, not a breakout into a new regime.

My read: the market is trading from “pricing imminent disaster” back toward “soft landing still possible.” That is a powerful short-term move, but it will only persist if the Hormuz reopening actually holds and the two-week pause does not become just a delayed strike clock.
