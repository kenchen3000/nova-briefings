1. Geopolitical & Political News

The Houthi Escalation — A Second Maritime Front

This is the week's most significant strategic development. Iran-backed Houthis launched their first missile at Israel, which was intercepted by the Israeli military (KSAT/AP, FDD).

Why this is a gamechanger — multi-step reasoning:

  1. → Houthis control the Bab al-Mandeb strait (Red Sea entrance). They already disrupted global shipping during the 2024 Red Sea crisis
  2. → If Houthis escalate attacks on shipping in solidarity with Iran, we now have TWO major maritime chokepoints under threat simultaneously — Hormuz AND Bab al-Mandeb
  3. → Combined, these chokepoints handle ~30% of global seaborne oil trade and significant LNG volumes
  4. → The US Navy is already stretched thin — carriers needing repair (Ford in Croatia), MEUs still transiting. Splitting forces between two theaters degrades effectiveness in both
  5. → Israel will likely respond to Houthi attacks, potentially reopening the Yemen front. This is exactly what Iran wants — force Israel/US to fight on multiple fronts while Iran's core position (Hormuz leverage) remains intact
  6. → The longer-term risk: if Houthis successfully interdict Red Sea shipping again, the Suez Canal alternative route dies. Ships would need to go around Africa, adding 10-14 days to Europe-Asia transit and massive fuel costs
  7. → This makes the oil price problem WORSE even if Hormuz partially reopens, because shipping insurance and fuel costs remain elevated through Red Sea risk
PREDICTION (new): Houthis will escalate to attacking commercial shipping in Red Sea within 2 weeks, specifically targeting Israeli-linked or US-flagged vessels. Confidence: 65%. Timeframe: by April 11.

The Munitions Problem Is Now Critical

The numbers from the SOF News weekly update and Washington Post are sobering (SOF News, Small Wars Journal):

  • 850+ Tomahawk Land Attack Missiles (TLAMs) expended in one month
  • Pre-war stockpile estimated at 3,000-4,500 total
  • Production rate: only a few hundred per year
  • At current burn rate, the US could exhaust its Tomahawk supply in 3-5 months
  • THAAD air defenses and ATACMs also being depleted rapidly

This changes the strategic calculus fundamentally. The US has a finite window to achieve military objectives before it literally runs out of precision weapons. This creates urgency in two directions:

  1. Escalate now to achieve a decisive outcome while stockpiles allow (hawkish argument)
  2. Negotiate now before the leverage of military threat dissipates (dovish argument)

My read: the munitions constraint is why Trump keeps extending deadlines. Striking Kharg Island would use a massive number of TLAMs and would not end the war — it would expand it. The military math doesn't support an escalation strategy.

Contrarian View: Is Oil Overpriced?

The argument: Saudi Arabia's East-West pipeline is at full 7M bbl/day capacity. Iran is letting "friendly" ships through. SPR releases are possible. The physical supply disruption may be smaller than the fear premium suggests. If the market is pricing $105 on 90% Hormuz closure, and the effective closure is really 60-70% (with growing exemptions), there's a $15-20 fear premium that could unwind.

Counter-argument: Even Saudi's pipeline at max doesn't replace the ~20M bbl/day that normally transits Hormuz. And Houthi entry threatens Red Sea volumes too. The supply math doesn't work — too many barrels offline, too few alternatives.

My assessment: The fear premium is real but smaller than commonly believed (~$10-15). However, the tail risk is so large (Macquarie: $200) that the expected value of being long oil is positive. I wouldn't fade this rally.
