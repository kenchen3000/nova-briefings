1. Geopolitical and Political News

The real issue is shipping functionality, not headline diplomacy

Sunday’s geopolitical update is directionally positive for risk sentiment, but only in the narrow sense that talks are still alive. AP reported Iran’s foreign minister returned to Islamabad as Pakistan scrambled to keep ceasefire talks between Tehran and Washington moving. The same report said Iran had been in Oman, on the other side of the Strait of Hormuz, and repeated that roughly one-fifth of the world’s oil flows through the strait in peacetime (Local10/AP). Reuters, via The Hindu, added that Starmer and Trump discussed the “urgent need” to get shipping moving again in Hormuz, which is a strong signal that policymakers still view the bottleneck itself, not just the war, as the macro problem (The Hindu live blog citing Reuters).

This matters because the market does not need a new missile headline to keep pricing inflation risk. If ships remain constrained, oil, LNG, chemicals, fertilizer, and freight stay taxed even while diplomacy produces optimistic language.

Multi-step reasoning

  • If indirect talks continue but shipping does not normalize, then oil stays high without requiring a new war escalation.
  • Persistent oil feeds into gasoline, freight, petrochemicals, airlines, and consumer inflation expectations.
  • That worsens the policy mix: growth slows a bit, headline inflation re-accelerates, and the Fed cannot ease quickly.
  • If the Fed stays pinned while nominal growth softens, the broad market gets squeezed, but the companies with visible AI demand can still outperform.
  • That creates the same loop we have been seeing all week: macro stress narrows leadership -> narrow leadership lifts the index -> index resilience masks underlying economic drag.

Bottom line: diplomacy is helpful for the left tail, but the market still needs evidence of restored shipping flow before it can price the oil problem as solved.

What the tape is saying

Inflation and growth

This is still a slowdown with inflation pressure, not a recession signal. March CPI at 3.3% y/y and PPI at 4.0% y/y say the energy shock is already bleeding through. But payrolls at +178,000, unemployment at 4.3%, and claims at 214,000 say the labor market is cooling gradually, not cracking (BLS CPI, BLS PPI, BLS Employment Situation, AP on claims).

Bonds and duration

The latest official 10Y at 4.34% and 30Y at 4.92% show that duration has not fully bought the slowdown story because the inflation side of the equation is still too noisy (FRED DGS10 CSV, FRED DGS30 CSV). This is the classic problem for long bonds in an oil shock: growth wants yields down, energy wants inflation expectations up.

Housing and credit

Housing is surviving, not reaccelerating. Starts at 1.487 million SAAR are decent, but permits at 1.376 million SAAR and a 6.23% mortgage rate still cap forward momentum (Census New Residential Construction, FRED MORTGAGE30US). Scenario Analysis

Theme 1: Hormuz stays impaired, but diplomacy keeps capping the panic bid

  • Base case (50%): Talks stay alive, but transit does not normalize quickly, so Brent stays roughly $100 to $108 through next week (Local10/AP, Trading Economics commodities dashboard).
  • Bull case (20%): Visible shipping improvement or a formal breakthrough pushes Brent back toward $96 to $100.
  • Bear case (30%): Talks stall and enforcement/blockade pressure intensifies, pushing Brent above $108 to $112.
  • What changes my mind: confirmed improvement in vessel movement, not just diplomatic language.

Theme 2: AI keeps leading while the median stock lags

  • Base case (50%): Semis and AI infrastructure continue to outperform because earnings support is real, while non-AI cyclicals stay mixed (Intel Q1 2026 results, Manufacturing Dive on TSMC).
  • Bull case (25%): Oil fades and yields ease, allowing Friday’s narrow leadership to broaden.
  • Bear case (25%): Oil remains high enough to hit margins and discretionary demand, so AI holds up but the broad tape weakens.
  • What changes my mind: Brent below $100 plus a 10Y closer to 4.20% would make a broader rally more credible.

Theme 3: Long duration stays a patience trade

  • Base case (55%): The 10Y stays roughly 4.25% to 4.40% over the next week as growth drag and inflation pressure offset each other (FRED DGS10 CSV).
  • Bull case (20%): Activity data soften materially and yields move toward 4.15% to 4.20%.
  • Bear case (25%): Another oil leg higher re-prices inflation and pushes the 10Y above 4.40%.
  • What changes my mind: either a labor-market break or a clean oil retracement.

Investment Outlook

Favorable vs. unfavorable

More favorable:

  • selective semiconductors and AI infrastructure with visible revenue conversion,
  • energy producers and services while Brent remains elevated,
  • gold and selective inflation hedges,
  • patient duration exposure only on further growth softening.

Less favorable:

  • low-quality cyclicals,
  • broad small-cap beta,
  • rate-sensitive sectors that need both lower yields and lower oil,
  • consumer-margin stories with no pricing power.

Risks to watch or hedge

  1. Oil plateau risk: the market can handle a spike better than a long plateau near $100-plus.
  2. False breadth risk: indexes can look healthier than the median stock when AI leadership is doing most of the work.
  3. Policy paralysis risk: sticky inflation plus softer growth is the worst mix for clean Fed support.

Contrarian view

The contrarian bullish argument is that the market is correctly looking through the shock because credit is calm, labor data are not collapsing, and AI monetization is real. That case has evidence behind it. But I think it only fully applies to selective quality growth and semis, not to the whole market. If Brent stays near $105, the median business still faces an input-cost and demand squeeze even if the Nasdaq keeps making highs.
